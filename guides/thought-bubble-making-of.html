In one of Thought Bubble's stories, a boy named Max is digging on a beach: Max dug a hole. Using the app's swap panel, we nudged the model's thinking — take out the idea of a hole, put in the idea of a castle — and let it rewrite the story. It never used the word castle. It wrote moat: the castle-thing you dig.

That small moment is the whole point of this app. The model didn't match a word; it carried an idea, and the idea shaped what came next. Thought Bubble exists so that a child — or you — can watch that happen, and poke at it.

What a language model actually does

A language model is a program trained on lots of text to do one deceptively simple thing: predict the next little piece of text (a token — a word or part of one). Write with it in a loop — predict, append, repeat — and stories come out.

"Just predicting the next word" sounds shallow. The catch is how the prediction is made. Between reading and answering, the text flows through a stack of processing layers. At each layer, for each token, the model maintains a long list of numbers — a kind of working thought. Early layers hold something close to the raw words; by the last layer the thought has sharpened into the next word. Everything interesting happens in between — and "in between" is normally invisible.

Lenses: making the invisible legible

In 2020, a researcher writing as nostalgebraist tried something charmingly direct, called the logit lens: take the model's half-finished thought at some middle layer and push it through the model's own final say-a-word machinery, as if the model had stopped thinking right there. The result: readable snapshots of a thought in progress — often meaningful surprisingly early.

The logit lens has a known flaw: middle layers don't speak quite the same "language" as the final layer, so the raw projection can distort. Later work (the tuned lens, Belrose and colleagues, 2023) fixed this by training a small translator for each layer. Thought Bubble's J-lens is in this family: a per-layer translator fitted to this specific story-writing model. The app keeps the plain logit lens too, as the "blurt-out" comparison view — and its DIFF mode shows exactly what the trained lens sees that the naive one misses: the model's quiet anticipations, drawn in green.

The research behind it, in plain terms

This little instrument is a scale model of a serious research field: interpretability — the science of understanding what is going on inside neural networks, rather than only measuring what comes out. Anthropic, the AI-safety company behind Claude, has made it a central bet, sometimes describing the work as doing biology on a grown organism nobody hand-designed. Three findings are especially relevant to what you see in Thought Bubble:

  • Thoughts decompose into features. In 2023–2024, Anthropic researchers showed that the tangled activity inside a model can be unmixed into millions of features — directions of activity that track concepts, from "the Golden Gate Bridge" to "code with a bug in it" ("Towards Monosemanticity," then "Scaling Monosemanticity" on Claude 3 Sonnet). Thought Bubble's readout chips are a kid-sized cousin: directions in the model's thought-space, translated to words.
  • Steering thoughts changes behavior. In a famous 2024 demo, Anthropic turned the Golden Gate Bridge feature up inside Claude — and for a day, "Golden Gate Claude" steered nearly every conversation toward the bridge. Thought Bubble's swap panel is precisely this kind of intervention in miniature: push the thought, not the text, and watch the story bend. Same-seed rewrites make it a controlled experiment a child can read.
  • Models plan ahead. In 2025, Anthropic's circuit-tracing work ("On the Biology of a Large Language Model") showed that when Claude writes a rhyming couplet, it often settles on the rhyme word first, then writes the line that arrives there. That is exactly what Thought Bubble's DIFF view and heat strip let you catch in a tiny model: words the model is getting ready to say, sentences early — and the moment it decided.
Why does any of this matter? Because if we're going to trust AI systems with consequential work, "it usually behaves well" isn't enough — we want to see the machinery, audit it, and correct it. Interpretability is how that becomes possible. An eight-year-old with a heat strip asking "where did it decide?" is practicing the exact question the field exists to answer.

Why a tiny model can carry big ideas

Thought Bubble's writer is stories110M — about 110 million parameters, thousands of times smaller than frontier models. It comes from a lovely 2023 result: Microsoft researchers (Eldan & Li, TinyStories) showed that if you train small models on simple synthetic children's stories, they learn to write coherently — tiny minds with a complete, child-sized world. Andrej Karpathy's open-source llama2.c project trained and published this checkpoint. Small enough to run in a web page, real enough to have genuine hidden thoughts: the perfect lab animal for a classroom observatory.

How the app itself is built

  • One file, no server. The whole instrument is a single HTML page. The model runs in your browser using web workers (a small pool, so the page stays responsive), and everything is cached locally after the first visit — stories never leave your machine.
  • An honest UI. The "raw" toggle shows the unfiltered lens; the strength slider marks the sane range in green; rewrites use the same random seed so the only variable is your swap. Where the instrument is approximate, the app says so.
  • Maggie, the guide who never grabs the controls. The voice layer (a reusable "voice-guide" core plus a per-app adapter) gives tours, answers questions, and can point at anything — but story-changing actions always need the visitor's explicit go-ahead. Getting an AI guide's manners right turned out to be its own design problem: whose move is the analyze button? Is filling the swap boxes consent to press swap? (No.) The guide was itself built in collaboration with AI assistants, with automated test suites checking everything from consent dialogs to what Maggie is allowed to see.
  • Grounded answers. When Maggie answers a question, she receives a precise snapshot of the screen — the story, the selected token, the actual readout rows, the outcome of the last swap — so her explanations are anchored to what you're looking at, not to guesswork.
