Hi! I live inside Thought Bubble. It's my magnifying glass: it lets us peek at what a tiny robot writer is thinking before it says anything. Press my play button in the app and I'll show you around — or read on!

What is Thought Bubble?

Inside this app lives a very small robot that writes children's stories, one word at a time. Before it picks each word, lots of half-formed ideas float around in its head — like thought bubbles in a comic. Thought Bubble lets you see those bubbles. No other toy you own can do that.

Your missions

Mission 1 — wake it up. The robot needs its brain files first. Ask a grown-up to press the fetch button, or to download the big model file and drop it on the dotted box. The very first visit takes a while (it's building a word map) — after that it opens in seconds.
Mission 2 — give it a story. Press one of the story chips, like the one about Ben and the broken vase. Then press analyze. The robot reads the story while the magnifying glass watches its brain.
Mission 3 — click a word. The story appears in little pieces called tokens. Click any word. The readout panel fills with the words the robot was getting ready to say right at that spot — maybe now, maybe a few sentences later. It thinks ahead, just like you do!
Mission 4 — find the secret plans. Press the DIFF button. The green words are ideas the robot is quietly holding that it wasn't about to blurt out. Those are its secret plans.
Mission 5 — where did it decide? Click any word-chip in the readout. The story lights up wherever that idea was glowing in the robot's mind:

Ben broke his mom's favorite vase. He heard her coming.

Look along the glow and ask the detective question: where did it decide?

Mission 6 — swap a thought! This is the boldest trick. First click a word in the story — that's the anchor, where the rethinking starts. Type a word to take out and a word to put in (the list only offers words the robot knows). Then press swap & rewrite. The robot writes the story again twice — once normally, once with the swapped thought — so you can spot exactly what changed.
🔍
Maggie's favorite true story

Once, in a story that said Max dug a hole, we took out hole and put in castle. The robot wrote moat — the castle-thing-you-dig! It caught the idea of a castle, not just the word. That's what swapping thoughts can do.

Detective tips

  • The raw switch is the honest view — every little word-piece, nothing hidden. Messier, but true.
  • Pink chips are bits of names. Names get chopped into pieces, so L… means a word starting like Lily is on its mind.
  • Short stories make thin readouts. Let the robot keep writing with generate — the longer the story, the more it has on its mind.
  • Stuck or curious? Ask Maggie! Type in her panel (or use the microphone) and ask anything, like "why is castle green?"
