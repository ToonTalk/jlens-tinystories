What you're actually looking at

The writer inside Thought Bubble is a small AI language model called stories110M. It learned to write by reading millions of simple children's stories, and it writes the same way it learned: one token at a time, always picking what comes next. Tokens are the pieces it reads and writes — usually a word, sometimes just part of one (names like Lily get chopped into L + ily).

Here's the interesting part. The model doesn't jump straight to its next word. Inside, the text passes through twelve layers of processing, and at every layer, for every token, the model holds a big bundle of numbers — you can think of it as a thought in progress. Thought Bubble reads those bundles and translates them back into words. That translator is called the J-lens.

The two lenses, and DIFF

ViewWhat it shows
J-lensWords the model is getting ready to say — maybe as the very next word, maybe a few sentences later. Ideas being carried, not yet spoken.
logit lensWhat the model would blurt out if it stopped thinking at that layer and answered immediately.
DIFFOnly the words the J-lens sees that the blurt-out view misses — drawn in green. That's the layer's hidden anticipation: its quiet plans.

The app shows layers L4 to L8. Below L4 the readouts barely change; above L8 the two lenses agree — the thought has become the word.

The heat strip: where did it decide?

Click any chip in the readout and the story lights up wherever that word ranked high in the model's mind. It answers the best detective question there is: not what did it think, but when did it start thinking it. Often an ending is quietly decided sentences before it appears.

Swap: steering a thought

The swap panel doesn't edit the text — it edits the thinking. Here is exactly what happens when you press swap & rewrite:

  • The anchor. The token you clicked. Everything up to and including it stays exactly as written; the model rewrites everything after.
  • Two rewrites, same dice. The model writes the continuation twice with the same random choices — once untouched, once with your swap. So any difference between the two panes was caused by the swap. Nothing else changed.
  • The edit is a nudge, not a command. From the anchor on, the app pushes the model's thought-bundle away from the take-out idea and toward the put-in idea. The strength slider says how hard to push — stay in the green band; too hard and the story breaks.
  • It's self-gating. The push only bites where the take-out idea is actually active. Swapping out a word the model isn't thinking about does nothing — check the readout or the heat strip first.

Three experiments to run

1. Catch a plan forming. Analyze the vase story. Click the token right after vase. and switch to DIFF. Is hid or sorry already green — before Ben has done anything? Now use the heat strip on that word: where did the idea first glow?
2. A swap that does nothing (on purpose). Pick an anchor where your take-out word is not in the readout, and run the swap. The two rewrites should come out identical — you've just proven the edit is self-gating. Then move the anchor to where the word is hot and run it again.
3. Same dice, different worlds. Run one swap, read the two panes side by side, and find the exact word where they split apart. Everything before it is identical — that's the same-dice guarantee. Everything after is the swap's doing.

Honest instrument notes

  • The raw toggle shows everything the lens sees — word-pieces and punctuation included. The filtered view is friendlier; raw is truer.
  • The lens is a translation, not a mind-reading X-ray. It's trained to be good, but it can blur or miss things — especially on very short stories.
  • This is a tiny model. Swaps steer the drift of ideas; they can't guarantee a particular word shows up. When a swap barely changes anything, that's data too.
