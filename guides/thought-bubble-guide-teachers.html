💭 Thought Bubble
a guide for teachers & parents

What it teaches, how to run it, and what happens to the data.
Kid-facing guides: for kids (8–11) · for older kids (11–14) · background & making-of

What this is

Thought Bubble is a hands-on instrument for looking inside a small AI language model while it writes children's stories. The model (about 110 million parameters, trained on the TinyStories dataset) runs entirely in the browser. A trained "lens" translates the model's internal state at each layer back into words, so a child can see what the model is holding in mind at any point in a story — and, in the boldest feature, gently steer that thinking and watch the story change.

An optional voice guide — Maggie, a magpie detective — gives a spoken tour and answers questions. She points and explains; the experiments always stay in the child's hands.

What it teaches

  • AI systems have internal states. A language model isn't a lookup table; ideas form across layers before words appear. That single intuition inoculates against both "it's magic" and "it's just autocomplete."
  • Ideas precede words. The DIFF view shows plans the model holds sentences before writing them — the same thinking-ahead behavior researchers have documented in frontier models.
  • Claims about minds can be tested. The heat strip ("where did it decide?") and the controlled swap experiment (two rewrites, same random seed, one changed variable) are genuine experimental method in miniature.
  • Interpretability is a real field. Researchers at labs like Anthropic build grown-up versions of exactly these tools to audit and steer large models — see the background page.

Classroom activities

Spot the plan (15–20 min, ages 8+). Analyze the vase starter. Students click the token after vase., switch to DIFF, and write down two green words. Discussion: the model hasn't written what Ben does yet — how does it already "know"? Where else do we see plans before actions?
Where did it decide? (20–30 min, ages 9+). Generate a longer story, then pick its most interesting late word. Use the heat strip to trace when that idea first lit up. Students mark the "decision point" in the printed story. Discussion: did the ending cause the beginning, or the other way round?
The controlled experiment (30–40 min, ages 11+). Teams design a swap: pick an anchor, predict what will change, run it, compare the two rewrites (same dice — the only difference is the swap). Include one deliberate "null" run where the take-out word isn't in the readout, so the rewrites come out identical. Discussion: what made the difference between a swap that bit and one that didn't? This is variable control, prediction, and negative results in one sitting.

Discussion questions that work

  • Is what the lens shows the model's "real thoughts," or a translation? What's the difference?
  • If the model plans an ending early, is it deciding — or just leaning?
  • We steered the story's ideas without touching its words. Should anyone be allowed to do that to a big AI? Who checks?

  2. The model file is too big for GitHub Pages (~420 MB). The app's fetch button pulls stories110M.bin from Google Drive automatically; the three small files (tokenizer, lens, vocabulary) come from the hosting folder. If an automatic fetch is blocked, download the model from that link and drop it on the app's dotted box.
  3. First run builds a word map ("one-time setup: building the lens's word-directions") — a few minutes on a school laptop. Everything is then cached in the browser: revisits open in seconds, offline included. Budget one patient first session per machine.
  4. Works from file:// too — download everything and drop the four files.

Privacy & safety

  • The story model runs locally in the browser. No accounts, no telemetry; students' stories never leave the machine.
  • The tour is fully local. Maggie's question-answering is the one optional online feature: inside a Claude artifact it uses the built-in service, and elsewhere it needs an adult to configure an AI-provider key (the little key button). Questions sent to Maggie go to that provider; nothing else does. Without a key, the tour and every instrument still work — Maggie will say she can't answer live questions.
  • The model writes TinyStories-style content: simple, gentle children's fiction. It is still a text generator; spot-check anything you'll print.

Honest limitations

  • This is a ~110M-parameter model — a minnow. Its "thoughts" are real internal states, but shallow ones; don't generalize its simplicity to frontier systems.
  • The lens is a trained translation of hidden states, not ground truth. Treat readouts as strong evidence, not gospel — that caveat is itself worth teaching.
  • Swaps steer idea-drift; they don't guarantee a chosen word appears. Null results are normal and pedagogically precious.
